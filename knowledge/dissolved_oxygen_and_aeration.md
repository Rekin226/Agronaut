# Dissolved Oxygen (DO), Gasping, and the Dawn Danger

Low dissolved oxygen kills fish faster than almost anything else, and it is often missed because it
strikes at night.

## Targets
- Maintain **DO ≥ 5 mg/L** at all times; ≥ 6 mg/L is comfortable. Below ~3 mg/L is an emergency for most species.
- Warm water holds **less** oxygen. Tilapia and catfish systems run warm, so DO management is critical;
  trout (cold-water) need even higher DO and are very unforgiving.

## Why fish gasp at dawn (classic pattern)
At night there is no photosynthesis, but fish, bacteria, and plant roots all keep **respiring** and
consuming oxygen. DO therefore falls through the night and hits its **lowest point right before/at dawn**.
"Fish gasping at the surface in the morning" is the textbook signature of low DO, especially with heavy
stocking, warm water, or lots of solids/biofilm consuming oxygen.

## Likely causes of low DO
- Warm water + high stocking density.
- Weak aeration, or a single point of failure (one air pump, no backup).
- Poor surface agitation / weak water return (gas exchange happens at the surface).
- Heavy organic load (uneaten feed, accumulated solids) — decomposition burns oxygen.
- Pump or power failure overnight (see common-failures).

## Immediate actions
- Add or increase **aeration** immediately (air stones, more air, stronger surface agitation).
- Make the water return splash/break the surface to drive gas exchange.
- Reduce feeding until DO is restored (digestion and decomposition both consume O2).
- Remove accumulated solids and uneaten feed.
- Check that the circulation pump is actually running and moving water through the system.

## Prevention
- Size aeration for the warmest expected water, not the average.
- Keep a **battery-backup air pump** — a power cut on a warm night can wipe out a tank within hours.
- Don't overstock; don't overfeed; keep solids under control.
