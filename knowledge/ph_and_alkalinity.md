# pH, Alkalinity (KH), and pH Crashes

In aquaponics, pH almost always drifts **downward** over time. Knowing why prevents most pH emergencies.

## Why pH falls
Nitrification is an acidifying process: converting ammonia to nitrate consumes carbonate alkalinity and
releases hydrogen ions. A healthy, well-fed system steadily eats its buffer and the pH drops. This is
normal — it is not a leak or a meter fault.

## Alkalinity (KH) is the buffer, not pH
- **KH (carbonate hardness)** is the reservoir that resists pH change. When KH is exhausted, pH "crashes"
  suddenly (can fall a full point or more overnight).
- Keep KH around **4–6 dKH (~70–110 ppm as CaCO3)**. If you only own one extra test kit, make it KH.
- A falling pH with low KH is the warning sign of an imminent crash. Top up the buffer *before* it hits.

## The three-way pH compromise
- Nitrifying bacteria prefer higher pH (~7.0–8.0).
- Plants take up nutrients best around **5.5–6.5**; above ~7.0, **iron and other micronutrients lock out**
  (causing deficiency even when present).
- Fish tolerate a species range but dislike rapid change most of all.
- The common compromise is **~6.8–7.0** — slightly favouring the bacteria and fish while keeping
  micronutrients reasonably available.

## Raising pH / KH safely (the usual need)
- Use **carbonates/bicarbonates**, not just "pH up" acids-of-opposite. Good choices double as nutrients:
  - **Potassium bicarbonate** — raises KH and adds potassium (a nutrient plants often lack).
  - **Calcium carbonate / calcium hydroxide** — raises KH and adds calcium.
- A established practice (UVI) is to **alternate potassium and calcium bases** so you buffer pH while
  supplying both K and Ca that the plants need.
- Add small amounts, dissolved, and re-test after a few hours. Never dump a large dose at once.

## Lowering pH (less common)
- Usually only needed in brand-new systems with hard, high-pH source water — and it often settles on its
  own as the system matures and acidifies.
- If required, use food-grade acid (e.g. phosphoric or nitric) in tiny increments, never near the fish.
- Avoid hydrochloric/muriatic and avoid sudden swings.

## Symptoms that point to pH/KH
- Plants yellowing between veins (iron lockout) despite a cycled system → suspect pH too high.
- Fish stressed after a "fine yesterday" day, pH reading very low → suspect a KH-exhaustion crash.
- pH won't hold after adjustment → KH is too low; build the buffer, don't keep chasing pH.
