# Plant Nutrient Deficiencies in Aquaponics

Aquaponics reliably supplies nitrogen (as nitrate) but tends to run **short on a few specific nutrients**.
Knowing the usual suspects saves chasing the wrong fix.

## The three classic aquaponics shortfalls
- **Iron (Fe)** — the most common deficiency. Shows as **interveinal chlorosis on NEW/young leaves**
  (yellow leaf, green veins, starting at the top). Often it is not a lack of iron but **pH lockout above
  ~7.0**. Fix: lower pH toward 6.5–6.8 if high, and dose **chelated iron** (Fe-DTPA holds up to ~pH 7.0–7.5;
  Fe-EDDHA for higher pH). Fish-safe at normal rates.
- **Potassium (K)** — fish feed is low in K. Shows as **yellowing/browning/scorch at the margins of OLDER
  leaves**, weak stems, poor fruiting. Fix: supplement via **potassium bicarbonate** (also buffers pH) or
  potassium hydroxide used as a pH base — supplies K while you manage pH.
- **Calcium (Ca)** — shows as **distorted/burnt new growth, tip burn, blossom-end rot** in fruiting crops.
  Often a uptake/transpiration issue as much as a supply one. Fix: **calcium carbonate/hydroxide** as a pH
  base supplies Ca; ensure airflow/transpiration for fruiting plants.

## Read the pattern: old leaves vs new leaves
- **Old (lower) leaves affected first** → mobile nutrient: nitrogen, potassium, magnesium.
- **New (upper) leaves affected first** → immobile nutrient: iron, calcium, manganese, sulphur.
This single distinction narrows the diagnosis fast.

## Nitrogen
Usually plentiful in a fed system (it is the cycle's end product). General pale-green of the **whole/older**
plant can mean low N — common in lightly stocked or under-fed systems. Fix by feeding more / more fish,
not by dumping fertiliser salts that may harm fish.

## The golden rule: check pH first
Most "deficiencies" in a cycled system are **lockout**, not absence. Before adding anything, confirm pH is
in the 6.0–7.0 band where micronutrients stay available. Correcting pH often resolves the symptom with no
supplement at all.

## Fish-safe supplementation
- Prefer supplements that double as pH buffers (K and Ca bicarbonates/hydroxides) — you fix two things at once.
- Use **chelated** iron, not raw iron sulphate dumps.
- Add small amounts and observe; many horticultural fertilisers contain levels of copper, zinc, or boron
  that are fine for plants but toxic to fish. When unsure, dose low and watch the fish.
