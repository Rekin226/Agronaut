# Nitrogen Cycle, Cycling a New System, and Ammonia/Nitrite Spikes

The nitrogen cycle is the heart of an aquaponics system. Most "sudden fish problems" trace back to it.

## How it works
Fish waste + uneaten feed → **ammonia (NH3/NH4+)** → *Nitrosomonas* bacteria → **nitrite (NO2-)**
→ *Nitrobacter / Nitrospira* bacteria → **nitrate (NO3-)** → taken up by plants.
The bacteria live mostly in the biofilter and on every wetted surface. They need oxygen and time to build up.

## Target readings (established system)
- Ammonia (total): ~0 ppm (anything persistently above ~0.5 ppm is a problem)
- Nitrite: ~0 ppm (toxic even at low levels)
- Nitrate: ~5–150 ppm — this is plant food; rising nitrate means the cycle is working
- The un-ionized fraction of ammonia (NH3) is the truly toxic part, and it rises sharply with **higher pH and higher temperature**. The same total ammonia is far more dangerous at pH 8 than at pH 7.

## Cycling a new system ("new system syndrome")
A new system takes roughly **3–6 weeks** to cycle. Expect, in order: an ammonia spike, then a nitrite
spike as the first bacteria appear, then nitrite falls and nitrate climbs (cycle complete).
- Cycle BEFORE adding many fish, or with very few hardy fish and light feeding.
- Seed the biofilter from an established system (used media, filter squeezings) to speed it up dramatically.
- Test ammonia and nitrite daily during cycling.

## Ammonia spike — likely causes
- Biofilter not yet established (new system) or damaged (chlorine, antibiotics, lost bacteria after power/heat).
- Overfeeding or a dead fish decomposing.
- Too many fish added at once (bioload exceeds biofilter capacity).
- High pH making existing ammonia much more toxic.

## Ammonia spike — safe actions
- Stop or sharply reduce feeding (no feed = no new ammonia) for 24–48h.
- Do a partial water change (25–50%) with dechlorinated, temperature-matched water.
- Increase aeration (bacteria and fish both need O2; nitrification is oxygen-hungry).
- Do NOT add more fish. Do NOT chase pH up — lower pH makes ammonia less toxic.
- Remove any uneaten feed and dead fish; check the biofilter is getting flow and oxygen.

## Nitrite spike — extra note
Nitrite causes "brown blood disease" (it blocks oxygen transport). Beyond the actions above, adding a
small amount of **chloride** (e.g. a little non-iodized salt) protects fish — chloride competes with
nitrite uptake across the gills. Use sparingly: salt stresses many plants, so use the minimum and only
during an active nitrite emergency.

## When in doubt
Stable beats fast. Reduce feed, add aeration, do modest water changes, and let the biofilter catch up.
Avoid large sudden chemical corrections unless fish are actively dying.
